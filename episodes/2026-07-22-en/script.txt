Wednesday, July 22nd. Here is your pork industry news. Today we'll focus on animal disease and biosecurity, U.S. market prices and supply, tariffs and trade, and market developments across China and Europe.

Let's begin with disease and biosecurity. According to Pig333, African Swine Fever continued to spread across Europe in the first half of 2026. By country, Poland reported the most cases at 1,425, followed by Lithuania with 832, Italy with 823, Germany with 621, and Hungary with 491. In Poland, an outbreak was confirmed at a farm holding some 21,390 pigs, and together with the spread among wild boar, strengthening containment remains a challenge. This spread risks pushing up production costs and eroding export competitiveness. In Spain, following the domestic ASF case confirmed in November 2025, pork exports to third countries in the first quarter of 2026 fell 14 percent year on year to 337,059 tonnes. While shipments to China dropped sharply, exports to South Korea rose 53.1 percent to 64,741 tonnes.

Pork Business, citing the latest surveillance from the Swine Health Information Center, reports that the global risk of African Swine Fever and Foot and Mouth Disease has reached record levels. New ASF strains, L1C.2.45 and L1A.13.49, are driving outbreaks in North Carolina and the Midwest, raising concern about incursion into the United States. Worldwide, more than 1.2 million ASF outbreaks have been reported across over 71 countries, and more than 2.4 million pigs have been lost to date. A recombinant new strain identified in Asia may also reduce the effectiveness of existing genotype 2 vaccines and diagnostic tools, calling for a review of biosecurity and diagnostic systems. Recurrence in Germany's Saxony region and a suspected case in Kenya have also been reported.

Now to U.S. market prices and supply. According to the CME Daily Livestock Report, the pork carcass cutout value reached 101 dollars and 10 cents per hundredweight on the CME market on July 21st, and lean hog futures also rose. The USDA national base hog price stood at 98 dollars and 13 cents, and the CME lean hog index was 96 dollars and 16 cents as of July 17th. According to National Hog Farmer, the breeding herd as of June 1st shrank to 5.88 million head, the smallest since 2014, and ongoing supply tightening from rising PRRS and PED infection rates is likely to affect marketings heading into the fall. Carcass weights are running one to two pounds heavier than a year ago, so even with modest growth in head count, production is being supported on a tonnage basis. The USDA forecasts 2026 pork production up 1.2 percent year on year, and up 2.4 percent in the third quarter, with lower feed costs encouraging a second straight year of heavier marketings.

According to analysis from Rabobank, expansion of the U.S. sow herd is running behind plan due to higher capital costs, construction permitting hurdles, and disease pressure, and 2026 production growth is expected to be limited to 0.7 percent year on year. Pork Business lists the downside risks for the second half as supply tightening from higher infection rates, a sharp narrowing of futures margins, the expiry of the China tariff exemption, demand competition from expanded broiler output, and rising input and borrowing costs, noting that while profits remain positive, the cushion has narrowed significantly. The usual seasonal summer rise in hog prices is limited this year, held back by weak demand for bellies and hams and soft foodservice demand. AHDB likewise reports that the North American market is holding firm, supported by lower summer production and substitution demand from beef.

On to tariffs and trade. According to Pork Business, the National Pork Producers Council argues that China's import restrictions violate WTO rules. Exports to China in the first quarter of 2026 fell 9 percent year on year to 104,779 metric tonnes, and 20 percent by value to 222.9 million dollars. Because exports of parts such as feet and stomachs to China are worth roughly 10 dollars per U.S. hog, this loss directly affects producer returns. The current export tariff to China is 47 percent, of which 10 percent is the reciprocal tariff portion. Exports to China in 2025 fell 21 percent to 367,416 metric tonnes, and the Section 301 tariff exemption is set to expire at the end of 2026. Resolving the sanitary and phytosanitary barriers will require further negotiation.

USMEF issued a statement welcoming the U.S.-EU trade framework that took effect on July 1st, 2026. The agreement establishes, for the first time, a duty-free quota of 25,000 metric tonnes per year for U.S. frozen, salted, and processed pork, valid through the end of 2029. Also, in mid-July, Mexico fully lifted its import restrictions related to pseudorabies virus on U.S. pork offal. The basis was the World Organisation for Animal Health recognizing the Iowa case as an isolated incident. During the restriction period, U.S. exporters were losing roughly 7 million dollars per week. China still requires full inspection.

Now packer developments and policy. The USDA has proposed revising the line-speed cap rule for pork processing plants, which if enacted would allow greater processing capacity. Cost reductions from deregulation are expected, and together with Smithfield's planned 1.3 billion dollar new plant, industry consolidation could accelerate. The USDA also expanded heavy-metal testing to processed meat products from July 20th, and will begin regular reporting on mercury. The stated aim is not a food-safety crisis response but greater public-health transparency.

Now the China market. According to AHDB, China's pork market is in serious oversupply. First-quarter production rose 4.2 percent year on year while prices fell 27 percent, and the government is trying to support prices through reserve purchases. According to the South China Morning Post, after live hog prices fell to multi-year lows, the National Development and Reform Commission and the Ministry of Commerce moved to buy frozen pork for state reserves, potentially the second such purchase this year. Pig333 reports that China's pork and offal imports in the first quarter of 2026 fell by about 40 percent year on year. Brazil is cementing its position as the largest supplier, while Spain has retreated amid import restrictions tied to its ASF outbreak, and U.S. product is shut out by tariffs exceeding 57 percent. Rabobank's analysis says China is aiming to cut its sow herd by 1 million head to a level of 39 million, with imports declining in the first half before gradually recovering in the second.

Finally, the European and UK markets. According to Pig333, Spain's reference market price fell for a third consecutive time in mid-July, with a firm market turning sharply lower. The first-ever exports of French pork offal to China, achieved after three years of negotiation, are a positive development. According to AHDB, the EU pork market is seeing continued production growth while weak exports weigh on prices, with the carcass weighted-average price down 26.5 percent year on year. With Spain, Germany, and France accounting for 54 percent of EU production, exports are not keeping pace. The European Commission's weekly price report also shows the EU pork carcass weighted-average price at 160.1 euros per 100 kilograms in week 26, down 26.5 percent year on year. The Philippines re-approving Spanish pork imports, conditional on ASF regionalization, is a new export opportunity.

According to a report from AHDB's UK pork sector council, in the UK a combination of higher marketings and historically low EU prices has widened the gap between pork prices and production costs to unprecedented levels. A shortage of chicken supply may generate substitution demand for pork, and the council has decided on additional investment in television and social media marketing and a promotional campaign for the summer of 2027. Reduced supply within the EU has also driven a rebound in intra-EU prices since early July, with weekly prices rising for a second straight week in Germany, Spain, the Netherlands, and Belgium. Pig Progress analysis suggests that global pork production in 2026 will expand in the first half on EU recovery and higher Vietnamese output, but turn to contraction in the second half amid China's sow-herd cuts and Spain's ASF constraints, leaving the year roughly flat to slightly down. Rabobank's second-quarter 2026 report says the direct impact of the Middle East conflict is limited for now, but warns of medium to long-term spillover through transport and energy costs. Feed costs remain low, providing a cushion for production costs.

Other topics. National Hog Farmer reports that cash-market barrow and gilt prices fell 2.7 percent from the prior month to 64 dollars and 82 cents per hundredweight, while AHDB reports that the July average plant-delivered price reached 78 dollars and 20 cents per hundredweight, the highest since August 2022. Multiple outlets report that after the cutout average briefly topped 101 dollars, lean hog futures turned lower.

That's your pork industry news for today.
