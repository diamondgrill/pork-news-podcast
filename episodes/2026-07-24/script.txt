Friday, July 24th. Here is your pork industry news.

Today we're covering a wave of developments on the China trade front, a fresh round of African swine fever outbreaks in Europe and Asia, and the latest US price and export figures.

Let's start with trade and the China market. According to Pork Business, China has announced it will suspend retaliatory tariffs on US pork and agricultural products that it imposed after March 4th, 2025. The National Pork Producers Council called the move an important step toward restoring a market-driven business environment. China imported 893 million dollars worth of US pork and byproducts in 2025, making it the third-largest export market for US pork. The industry is still pushing for full tariff elimination and permanent market access, and how those negotiations unfold will shape export volumes going forward.

That tariff pause is already showing up in the trade data. US pork exports to China climbed 33 percent year over year in April 2026, marking a sharp rebound. Still, the first quarter of 2026 had been weak, with volume down 9 percent and value down 20 percent from a year earlier. So April's jump may only be a partial rebound rather than a full recovery. A lasting turnaround will depend on complete tariff removal and stable, predictable market access.

Even with that rebound, China's overall pork import appetite remains soft. A pig333 report found that China's pork imports fell 26.4 percent in the first half of 2026 compared with a year earlier. Rising domestic production and weak prices at home are curbing China's need for imported pork. Cold storage inventories in China are also running high, leaving little near-term room for a bounce in imports. That leaves major exporters, including the US, the EU, and Brazil, needing to find alternative markets.

A separate AHDB report on the Chinese market points to the same story from the supply side. Oversupply from rising domestic production and slow-to-recover consumer demand are weighing on Chinese pork prices. Cautious consumer spending and soft restaurant demand are compounding the problem, and AHDB doesn't see a quick market recovery. That means EU and US pork exports bound for China are likely to keep struggling in the near term, reinforcing the push toward market diversification.

Turning to US prices and exports directly. USMEF reported that US pork export volume held roughly steady at 221,809 metric tons, in line with a year ago. But export value climbed 20 percent to 657.3 million dollars. Variety meats were a standout, with 49,092 metric tons shipped, up 54 percent from last year. That variety meat volume was worth 116.7 million dollars, up 69 percent, the second-highest figure on record. Year-to-date, export volume is up 1 percent at about 1.8 million metric tons, and value is up 8 percent at 4.98 billion dollars. Both are running at record pace, which USMEF credits to the industry's market diversification strategy.

On the domestic price side, PorkCheckoff's weekly summary for the week of July 17th is out. It put the pork carcass cutout value at 101.23 dollars per hundredweight, down 42 cents from the prior week. Ribs and hams gained on the week, while loins, butts, picnics, and bellies all slipped. Prices have followed a gentle softening trend through the period. Year-to-date, pork prices are still running 2.6 percent above last year, and the full-year outlook continues to call for a 1.9 percent increase. Supply and demand mismatches, along with seasonal shifts, are shaping the near-term price path.

Not every export market is cooperating, though. A USMEF audio report from July 13th covered Mexican restrictions on pork variety meat imports, tied to concerns about pseudorabies virus. Those restrictions dealt a real blow to May 2026 US pork exports. Mexico is one of the largest markets for US pork and carries strong demand for variety meats, so the impact has been significant. USMEF says it continues talks with Mexican authorities and has questioned the scientific basis for the restrictions. By contrast, US beef exports are running ahead of last year, leaving pork as the outlier struggling under this particular restriction.

Now to animal health, where African swine fever activity picked up on several fronts this week. In Spain, African swine fever has been confirmed in wild boar near Barcelona, the first such case in the country since 1994. At least eleven boar carcasses have since tested positive for the virus. Spain is one of the EU's largest pork exporters, so any spread into commercial herds could hit key export markets hard. The situation could also have an immediate effect on trade talks with countries that may respond with their own import restrictions.

A broader global surveillance report as of July 2026 found African swine fever and foot-and-mouth disease cases at record levels worldwide. More than 71 countries have now confirmed over 1.2 million African swine fever cases, with more than 2.4 million farmed pigs lost since 2022. In Germany's North Rhine-Westphalia state, a new African swine fever strain traced to southern Italy was detected, suggesting a separate new introduction into the country. In Kenya, mass pig deaths in Kakamega County are also suspected to involve African swine fever. Altogether, supply risk to global pork production remains elevated, with implications for investment, insurance, and production planning.

South Korea, meanwhile, confirmed its first farm-level African swine fever outbreak of 2026. The infection occurred despite the country's strict biosecurity measures, which has raised concern across the industry there. Animal health authorities have started immediate investigations of nearby farms along with movement restrictions, while the scale of the spread is still being assessed. Across the wider Asia-Pacific region, more than 1,594 farms have now been affected and over 67,000 pigs have died. That underscores that African swine fever remains a serious ongoing threat in the region.

A pig333 review of how African swine fever evolved across Europe in the first half of 2026 ties several of these threads together. It highlights new regional introductions, including that first Spanish wild boar case since 1994 and the newly detected virus strain in Germany. The review underscores just how difficult African swine fever remains to manage across EU member states. Market access restrictions and export limits in affected regions are already having a direct business impact. The first-half picture will serve as an important benchmark for disease control and trade talks going forward.

Let's look at the broader supply outlook. A Pig Progress analysis expects global pork production in 2026 to rise slightly in the first half of the year before turning lower in the second half. USDA's June quarterly report showed record productivity on US farms, even as the US breeding herd fell to its smallest June 1st size since 2014. Disease risks from African swine fever, PED, and PRRSV, combined with feed cost trends, are expected to weigh on production. Add in China's steep drop in imports, and the global supply and demand balance is shifting in ways the whole industry is watching closely.

In Europe, the European Commission's meat market report put the average EU pig carcass price for week 26, grades S and E, at 160.1 euros per hundred kilograms. That was up slightly from the prior week, but down 1.9 percent over the past four weeks and down 26.5 percent from a year earlier. EU pork production is expected to fall 1 percent for the year to 21.7 million metric tons, as low prices and disease risk push producers to scale back. With Chinese import demand weak, EU producers are racing to find alternative markets, and any price recovery will depend on stronger demand elsewhere.

In the UK, the AHDB Pork Sector Council chair's July update said domestic pork consumption keeps growing, alongside rising exports to international markets. Higher clean pig throughput pushed UK production up 2.3 percent year over year to 975,200 tons. At the same time, rising feed and other production costs are squeezing producer margins against pig prices. Continued growth will depend on stable pricing and further export diversification.

Finally, a policy story out of Washington. Democratic Senator Chuck Schumer introduced the Family Grocer and Farmer Relief Act. It would direct the Federal Trade Commission to address consolidation in the meatpacking industry. The bill takes aim at major packers including Smithfield, Tyson, and JBS, and could push the industry toward restructuring if it passes. Those three companies already handle more than half of domestic cattle and hog processing, and industry concentration has become a political flashpoint. How Congress moves on tighter regulation will directly affect packers' investment plans going forward.

That's your pork industry news for today.
