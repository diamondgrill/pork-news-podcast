Thursday, July 23rd. Here is your pork industry news.

Today we cover strong U.S. hog prices amid tight breeding herds, and a warning about softening global prices ahead. We also look at African swine fever spreading in Germany and Poland, plus trade developments touching China, Canada, and Mexico.

Let's start with prices and supply here in the United States. According to PorkCheckoff, the USDA's June Quarterly Hogs and Pigs report showed the total U.S. hog inventory at 73 point 66 million head, roughly flat with last year. The breeding herd, however, fell to its lowest level since 2014. Record productivity is offsetting that tighter breeding herd. Farms averaged 11 point 87 pigs per litter. Farrowing intentions for June through August came in at 2 point 9 million head, down 2 point 2 percent from a year ago. That points to tighter pork supplies ahead, and the report was seen as more bullish than the market expected. PorkCheckoff says supply should stay tight and prices should stay elevated in the near term, with producer margins looking relatively favorable.

Market data released this week showed the pork cutout climbing to 104 dollars and 82 cents per hundredweight on Tuesday, July 22nd. That was up one dollar and 72 cents from the prior day, a bounce back after recent softness. The cash hog price rose to 100 dollars and 22 cents, up one dollar and 33 cents. CME October lean hog futures ticked up slightly to 88 point 125 cents per pound. Gains were led by bellies, ribs, and hams. Federal hog slaughter came in at 479,000 head for the day, bringing the weekly total to 910,000 head. The cutout moved back above the 100 dollar mark this week, defying recent talk that a summer rally was not coming.

According to AHDB, demand for U.S. pork is holding steady even as production growth stays limited, with that same low breeding herd behind the slower output. The cutout has been holding in the 100 dollar range, supported by summer grilling season demand. AHDB expects the third quarter producer price to come in around 75 dollars per hundredweight, with elevated prices likely to continue through the second half of the year.

National Hog Farmer reports that strong demand and tight supply should keep U.S. hog prices elevated through 2026. Quarterly producer price forecasts run 65 dollars in the first quarter, 73 dollars in the second, 75 dollars in the third, and 63 dollars per hundredweight in the fourth. Annual pork production is forecast to rise 2 point 5 percent to 28 point 3 billion pounds. Winter disease risk and limited appetite among producers to expand their breeding herds should support high prices through the first half of the year.

On the packer side, Pork Business reports that both beef and pork packer margins are being squeezed. Per-head profits are down sharply from both last week and a year ago. Pork packer margins have shrunk from more than 47 dollars per head a year ago to just a few dollars per head now, as rising live hog costs outpace product price gains. That is shifting negotiating leverage toward producers, and the industry is watching for effects on plant run rates and slaughter volumes.

Now let's turn to the global price picture, where the outlook is more mixed. Pig Progress reports warning signs flashing in the global pig price cycle. Oversupply trends in the European Union, China, and the United States are pressuring prices, and the risk of price declines is rising heading into late 2026 and into 2027. Europe in particular is seeing continued production growth alongside weak export demand, which could prolong the price adjustment. Pig Progress says producers there will need to focus on cost management and cutting expenses.

In the United Kingdom, AHDB's latest outlook projects 2026 pork production up 5 percent to about 1 point 03 million tonnes. First quarter production costs did fall, but net margins for UK producers remain negative. Weak EU prices continue to weigh on UK prices as well, squeezing competitiveness. On the positive side, domestic consumption and exports are both trending upward. AHDB also has four television advertising campaigns planned between October 2026 and October 2027 to help boost demand further.

National Hog Farmer reports that EU pig production is forecast to rise to 61 point 2 million head in the fourth quarter of 2026, up 3 point 2 percent from a year earlier. Spain leads with 15 point 8 million head, Germany is up 3 point 8 percent to 9 point 5 million head, and Denmark is up 3 point 1 percent. Meanwhile, EU cattle production is declining, which could shift some protein demand toward pork. That said, rising pig supply could also pressure prices, so export demand will be key to watch. Spain, Germany, and France together account for 54 percent of total EU pork production, underscoring how concentrated the region's output remains.

Turning to disease and biosecurity, Pig Progress reports African swine fever has hit a Polish pig farm, affecting about 21,000 pigs. At the same time, Germany is seeing a surge in African swine fever cases among wild boar. Poland is a major EU pork exporter, so a confirmed farm outbreak raises concerns about tighter export restrictions and price impacts. This comes as the European pork market is already under pressure from oversupply, adding another layer of uncertainty.

Separately, Pig Progress reports that wild boar African swine fever cases in the German state of North Rhine-Westphalia have now passed 500. That state is a high pig-density, heavily industrial farming region in western Germany, so the rising wild boar case count raises the risk of the virus spreading onto farms. Continued African swine fever expansion in a major producing region could extend the negative impact on German pork trade. Export restrictions and lower buying premiums may follow, with knock-on effects for neighboring exporting countries.

Now to trade and tariffs. Pork Business reports that China has suspended the retaliatory tariffs on pork and other agricultural products that it imposed starting March 4th, 2025. That is a positive development for U.S. pork exports. Even so, first quarter 2026 U.S. pork exports to China remained down 9 percent, at 104,779 metric tons, with value down 20 percent to 222 point 9 million dollars. China is the third largest export market for U.S. pork and accounts for 59 percent of U.S. pork byproduct exports. Total exports to China were valued at about 893 million dollars in 2025, and industry groups are calling for continued diplomatic efforts to improve market access.

Pig Progress reports that Canadian pork producers are disappointed with a new U.S. voluntary country of origin labeling rule from USDA's Food Safety and Inspection Service, which took effect in January 2026. Under the rule, processed products made using Canadian-origin pork can no longer carry a "Product of USA" label. Canadian producers are concerned this could lead U.S. processing plants to reduce their purchases of Canadian hogs. The timing is notable, since the first six-year review of the CUSMA trade agreement formally began in July 2026. This labeling issue is expected to be a major point in those talks. A drop in Canadian hog shipments to the U.S. could put downward pressure on Canadian producer prices.

Finally, Pork Business reports that U.S. pork exports to Mexico are proving resilient despite restrictions tied to pseudorabies. Byproduct, or offal, exports have been affected by the restrictions, but exports of pork itself have held up as importers adapt and find substitute demand. Mexico remains one of the largest destinations for U.S. pork exports, making the stability of this trade relationship important for the industry. Future developments in pseudorabies-related regulations and ongoing talks between the two countries will be key to a fuller export recovery.

Rounding out the week, Pig Progress's July wrap-up ties these threads together. It points to expanding African swine fever in Europe, the start of the CUSMA review affecting Canadian pork trade, and rising EU production weighing on prices. Globally, supply and demand are gradually easing into balance, with downward price pressure showing up across several major producing regions. In North America, though, the low U.S. breeding herd continues to support prices, keeping that market comparatively strong.

That's your pork industry news for today.
